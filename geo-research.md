# How AI Search Systems Select and Cite Sources in June 2026

## Scope and evidence standard

This report separates four evidence grades. **Proven** means a claim is directly documented by the vendor or measured in a reproducible public study. **Strong evidence** means multiple independent empirical sources point in the same direction. **Hypothesis** means an inference from observed outputs, security experiments, or partial documentation. **Marketing claim** means a vendor or commercial tool says something that is not yet independently well verified. That distinction matters because, in June 2026, the public still sees only the outer behaviour of these systems, not their internal ranking and attribution code. citeturn8view0turn8view1turn8view5turn8view6turn12view5turn17search3

The strongest public evidence base is now reasonably broad. On the academic side, it includes: the original **GEO** paper from KDD 2024, which introduced visibility metrics for generative engines and experimentally modified page content in a benchmark setting; **How Generative AI Disrupts Search** (arXiv, 2026), which compared Google SERP, AI Overviews, and Gemini on a public benchmark of **11,500 queries** plus time-sensitive extensions; **Measuring Google AI Overviews** (arXiv, 2026), which issued **55,393 trending queries** over **40 days** and verified **98,020 atomic claims** in AIO outputs; **From Citation Selection to Citation Absorption** (arXiv, 2026), which analysed **602 controlled prompts**, **21,143 valid citations**, and **18,151 fetched pages** across ChatGPT, Google AIO/Gemini, and Perplexity; **News Source Citing Patterns in AI Search Systems** (arXiv, 2025), which used **24,000+ conversations**, **65,000 responses**, and **366,000+ citations** from Search Arena; and **Cited but Not Verified** (arXiv, 2026), which benchmarked **14 models** for citation accessibility, relevance, and factual support in deep-research style reports. Each is useful, but each also has limitations: some are platform snapshots, some use proxy metrics for “absorption”, and some evaluate API or browser surfaces rather than every product surface a user may see. citeturn29view0turn9view4turn38view4turn40view4turn10view4turn24view2

The strongest public industry datasets are narrower but still informative. Ahrefs published two especially useful measurement studies: one compared **15,000 prompts** across ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, and Perplexity against Google and Bing rankings; another analysed **863,000 SERPs** and **4 million AI Overview URLs** to estimate how often Google AIO citations came from top-ranking pages. Google also launched dedicated **Search Console** visibility reporting for generative AI features in June 2026, which means site owners now have some first-party measurement of impressions in AI Overviews and AI Mode, though not the full source-selection logic. citeturn13view1turn12view0turn12view3

## System findings

**ChatGPT Search**

**Retrieval mechanism — Proven.** OpenAI documents that ChatGPT Search is a search-enabled mode in ChatGPT that may trigger automatically or manually; it can rewrite the user’s prompt, query a search partner, and sometimes issue additional, more specific searches to other search providers. OpenAI also documents separate bots for different functions: **OAI-SearchBot** for search inclusion, **GPTBot** for model-training crawl, and **ChatGPT-User** for user-initiated page visits. That means OpenAI publicly distinguishes between content used for search surfacing and content used for foundation-model training. citeturn21view3turn21view0turn8view3

**Commonly cited sources — Strong evidence.** OpenAI says ChatGPT Search cites “news articles and blog posts” and uses partner or provider data for categories such as weather, stocks, sports, news, maps, and restaurants. In the public **geo-citation-lab** snapshot, ChatGPT’s citation pool was dominated by **official**, **news**, and **vertical** sources, which together made up **87.52%** of cited source types. That is not a universal law, but it is currently the clearest public cross-platform measurement of ChatGPT’s source mix. citeturn21view3turn22view0turn40view1

**Citation selection — Strong evidence.** OpenAI publicly exposes source links inline or in the Sources panel, but it does not disclose the internal ranking weights used to choose those citations. The best public measurement is descriptive: the geo-citation-lab dataset found ChatGPT triggered search on **579 of 587** observed prompts and returned a mean of **6.88 citations** per prompt in that controlled test. Tow Center’s journalism study adds an important qualification: citation _presence_ does not imply citation _accuracy_. In its March 2025 cross-platform test of **1,600 queries** across eight search agents, ChatGPT Search was part of a class of systems that frequently returned incorrect or fabricated citations when asked to identify quoted news articles. citeturn9view8turn16view0turn16view1

**Source absorption — Strong evidence, but proxy-based.** The most important public result for ChatGPT is that it appears **citation-sparse but absorption-heavy** in the April 2026 geo-citation-lab analysis. ChatGPT cited fewer pages than Google AIO/Gemini or Perplexity, but the cited pages had much higher mean **influence_score** in the final answers: **0.2713** for ChatGPT versus **0.0584** for Google and **0.0646** for Perplexity. The limitation is crucial: the paper explicitly says **influence_score is a constructed proxy**, not a direct trace of model attention or causal contribution. So the supported claim is not “ChatGPT definitely reads sources more deeply”, but “public output patterns are consistent with deeper per-source reuse in this snapshot”. citeturn40view0turn9view9

**Ranking versus citation differences — Strong evidence.** ChatGPT citations overlap only weakly with standard SERP rankings. Ahrefs’ **15,000-prompt** study found that, on average, only **12%** of URLs cited by ChatGPT, Gemini, and Copilot appeared in Google’s top 10 for the same prompt; ChatGPT’s own overlap was around **8%** for in-text citations and **6.1%** for bottom references in that dataset. That supports a strong practical conclusion: ranking in a traditional SERP and being cited by ChatGPT are related, but far from equivalent. citeturn13view1turn13view5

**Trust signals, authority, freshness, structure, brand, schema, reviews, backlinks — Mixed evidence.** What is **proven** for ChatGPT is mostly eligibility and freshness plumbing: OpenAI says public websites can appear if they are not blocking **OAI-SearchBot**, and it separates search crawling from training crawling. What is **strong evidence** from cross-platform measurement is that pages with stronger **semantic alignment**, clearer **modular structure**, more **headings**, and more extractable evidence such as **definitions, figures, comparisons, and procedures** are more likely to be strongly “absorbed” into answers. What remains mostly **hypothesis** for ChatGPT Search specifically is the weight of **backlinks, reviews, brand mentions, or schema markup**. No public OpenAI document quantifies those weights, and no reproducible external study isolates them causally for ChatGPT Search alone. citeturn22view2turn8view3turn40view3turn40view4

**Academic papers and datasets — Strong evidence.** Relevant public work includes GEO (KDD 2024), Search Arena and its news-citation follow-on analysis, the 2026 citation-selection/absorption paper, and the Tow Center attribution studies. The best public datasets are **Search Arena** with **24,069 conversations**, **65,000 responses**, and **12,652 human preference votes**; **geo-citation-lab** with **602 prompts** and **21,143 citations**; and Ahrefs’ **15,000-prompt** overlap study. citeturn29view0turn14search2turn10view4turn10view2turn13view1

**Well-supported claims.** ChatGPT Search uses live web retrieval with query rewriting and multiple providers; inclusion in search is distinct from training crawl; official/news/vertical sources dominate in current public datasets; and citation overlap with Google top rankings is low. Publisher licensing or crawler access also does **not** guarantee accurate attribution, according to Tow Center’s experiments. citeturn21view0turn8view3turn40view1turn13view1turn16view1

**Speculative or unsupported claims.** The public does **not** know the exact weighting of authority, backlinks, reviews, brand mentions, or structured data in ChatGPT Search citation selection. It also does not know how often an uncited answer statement came from retrieval versus model memory versus partner feeds. Claims that any one “hack” reliably improves citation rates in ChatGPT Search are therefore unsupported. citeturn8view3turn22view2turn40view4

**ChatGPT Deep Research**

**Retrieval mechanism — Proven.** OpenAI documents deep research as an **agentic** process: the system plans a research workflow, can search the web, use uploaded files and connected apps, and can use code execution for analysis. In the API, deep research requires at least one data source such as **web search**, **remote MCP servers**, or **file search**; the response can expose intermediate **web_search_call** steps and final inline citations with source metadata and character spans. citeturn8view1turn8view2turn23view0

**Commonly cited sources — Proven in mechanism, limited in distribution.** Deep research can cite websites, uploaded files, and connected applications, depending on what the user allows. That is publicly documented. What is **not** publicly documented is a large-scale distribution of which websites or domains Deep Research cites most often in the consumer product. citeturn8view1turn23view0

**Citation selection — Strong evidence on interface, limited evidence on ranking.** OpenAI exposes citations, source metadata, and activity history, so the _citation interface_ is clear. What is not disclosed is the internal logic for selecting which of many visited sources become final inline citations. The best adjacent public evidence comes from **Cited but Not Verified**: across **14 models** in deep-research style pipelines, working links and topical relevance were common, but factual support for attributed claims varied much more. That suggests citation appearance can overstate evidentiary quality in research agents generally. The limitation is that this benchmark evaluates citation-rich research agents, not only OpenAI’s consumer Deep Research product. citeturn8view1turn23view0turn24view2

**Source absorption — Strong evidence for agentic research generally, limited product-specific evidence.** The strongest public result here is not vendor-specific but workflow-specific: in the same 2026 source-attribution paper, **Fact Check** accuracy dropped by about **42% on average** as search depth rose from **2 to 150 tool calls**, while link validity and relevance stayed high. That implies more retrieved material does not necessarily mean better factual synthesis. For OpenAI Deep Research itself, the API’s exposure of search steps and code-execution steps makes heavy retrieval and synthesis **proven**, but the exact relationship between number of sources and true answer dependence remains unobserved outside OpenAI. citeturn24view2turn23view0

**Ranking versus citation differences — Hypothesis supported by mechanism.** Deep Research is not a single-SERP summariser; it is a multi-step research workflow that can refine queries, branch, revisit sources, and combine web content with private files. Because of that design, standard “does the citation rank in Google’s top 10 for the original query?” is often the wrong measurement. That is a sound inference from the documented workflow, but I did not find a public large-scale overlap study specific to ChatGPT Deep Research. citeturn8view1turn23view0

**Trust signals, authority, freshness, structure, schema, reviews, backlinks — Mixed evidence.** OpenAI’s cookbook explicitly recommends prioritising **reliable, up-to-date sources** such as peer-reviewed research, regulators, and official data for research tasks. Cross-platform absorption work also suggests that pages with clearer headings, higher semantic alignment, and dense extractable evidence are more likely to influence generated answers. But there is no public OpenAI evidence quantifying the role of backlinks, reviews, brand mentions, or general schema markup in Deep Research source choice. citeturn23view0turn40view3

**Academic papers and datasets — Limited but growing.** The strongest directly relevant paper is **Cited but Not Verified** (2026), which benchmarked deep-research style attribution quality across 14 models. More general supporting work comes from GEO and citation-absorption studies, but those are not specific to OpenAI Deep Research as a consumer product. No large public dataset currently isolates Deep Research outputs at the same scale that Search Arena isolates AI search conversations. citeturn24view2turn40view4

**Well-supported claims.** Deep Research is an agentic, multi-step retrieval-and-synthesis workflow with citation metadata, source controls, and auditable search steps. Cited links in research-agent outputs can be highly accessible and topically relevant while still failing stricter factual support checks. citeturn8view1turn23view0turn24view2

**Speculative or unsupported claims.** Which kinds of web sources Deep Research systematically prefers at scale, how strongly it rewards brand authority, and how much answer text comes from model memory versus retrieved evidence are not publicly knowable. citeturn8view1turn23view0

**Perplexity**

**Retrieval mechanism — Proven.** Perplexity is the most transparent of the major vendors on retrieval architecture. Its 2025 research article says its production search infrastructure handles **200 million daily queries**, indexes **200+ billion unique URLs**, and combines **hybrid retrieval**, **multi-stage ranking**, and dynamic content parsing. Its developer docs distinguish between a **Search API** that returns raw ranked results from a continuously refreshed index and **Sonar** / agent APIs that generate prose answers with citations. Perplexity also publicly documents separate bots: **PerplexityBot** for search indexing and **Perplexity-User** for user-requested fetches. citeturn18view0turn17search3turn35view1

**Commonly cited sources — Strong evidence.** In geo-citation-lab, Perplexity’s citations were dominated by **official**, **news**, and **vertical** sources, with official sources accounting for **44.07%** and those three classes together making up **79.12%** of the cited mix. Perplexity’s own product positioning—“answer engine” with citations on nearly every answer—is real as interface design, but should still be treated separately from empirical proof of citation quality. citeturn40view1turn13view2

**Citation selection — Strong evidence.** Perplexity’s API response schema explicitly returns both `citations` and `search_results`, and also reports `num_search_queries`, which is unusually informative. In geo-citation-lab, Perplexity had the broadest citation behaviour of the three major platforms studied, averaging **16.35 citations** per prompt across **602** prompts. Search Arena’s news-citation study further shows that Perplexity, OpenAI, and Google do not cite the same outlets, even when they exhibit some shared concentration patterns. citeturn35view0turn9view8turn25view0

**Source absorption — Strong evidence, proxy-based.** The same 2026 absorption paper found that Perplexity cited the most sources but had relatively low mean per-source influence (**0.0646**) compared with ChatGPT. The supported public claim is therefore not that Perplexity’s sources matter less, but that its behaviour is **breadth-heavy**: it attaches many citations, while any one cited page appears to exert less measured influence on the final answer. The proxy limitation again applies. citeturn40view0turn40view2

**Ranking versus citation differences — Strong evidence.** Among the major assistants in Ahrefs’ **15,000-prompt** study, Perplexity aligned **closest** to Google rankings, with **28.6%** of cited URLs appearing in Google’s top 10 for the same query. That still means the majority of Perplexity citations did **not** come from Google’s top 10, which is an important correction to simplistic “AI just cites rankings” claims. Perplexity’s own docs also state that it uses its **own** search index rather than Google’s or Bing’s. citeturn13view1turn17search3turn35view1

**Trust signals, authority, freshness, structure, schema, reviews, backlinks — Mixed evidence.** Perplexity is explicit about aiming for **completeness, freshness, and speed**, and about using hybrid retrieval plus fine-grained content understanding. Cross-platform evidence suggests pages that are longer, better structured, semantically aligned, and rich in definitions, numbers, comparisons, and procedures are more likely to meaningfully shape answers. What remains unproven publicly is whether classic SEO signals like backlinks, reviews, or schema markup have any direct independent weight in Perplexity’s final citation selection. citeturn18view0turn40view3

**Academic papers and datasets — Strong evidence.** Useful public resources include Perplexity’s own architecture article, **GEO**, **geo-citation-lab**, **Search Arena**, the news-citation paper built on Search Arena, and the ICLR 2025 paper **Adversarial Search Engine Optimization for Large Language Models**, which successfully manipulated Perplexity and Bing outputs using crafted webpage content. That last result is not a normal ranking signal study, but it is strong evidence that source selection can be influenced by on-page content patterns in at least some circumstances. citeturn18view0turn29view0turn10view2turn14search2turn27search1

**Well-supported claims.** Perplexity uses its own hybrid search stack; returns many citations; overlaps more with Google top rankings than ChatGPT or Gemini, but still not predominantly; and is vulnerable, at least in published experiments, to manipulative on-page content. citeturn18view0turn35view0turn13view1turn27search1

**Speculative or unsupported claims.** Exact weighting of authority/backlinks/reviews/schema remains undisclosed. Claims that Perplexity “rewards schema” or “prefers Reddit reviews” as a stable rule are not publicly established by reproducible causal evidence. citeturn17search3turn40view4

**Google AI Overviews and AI Mode**

**Retrieval mechanism — Proven.** Google is now unusually explicit. Search Central states that generative AI features in Search are rooted in Google’s core ranking and quality systems and rely on **retrieval-augmented generation** from the Search index plus **query fan-out**. Google’s AI Mode help page says AI Mode divides a question into subtopics and searches for each simultaneously. That is the clearest official public statement of retrieval mechanics among the major vendors. citeturn12view5turn20view0

**Commonly cited sources — Strong evidence.** Google’s own docs say AI Overviews surface links to help users explore the web. The strongest public measurement study found that AIO-cited domains were, on average, more credible than co-displayed first-page results and less reliant on unvetted UGC. In that March–April 2026 dataset, the most frequently cited AIO hosts were **youtube.com**, **en.wikipedia.org**, **facebook.com**, **instagram.com**, and **usatoday.com**. That is a measured pattern, not an official policy statement. citeturn8view6turn39view3

**Citation selection — Strong evidence.** The 2026 AIO measurement study is the strongest public source here. It issued **55,393** trending queries over **40 days**, observed **7,583** AIOs, and found that AIO activation was **13.7%** overall but **64.7%** for question-form queries. It also found that AIO citation selection is not simply equivalent to first-page ranking: only **25.0%** of AIO reference domains overlapped the top 5 first-page results, **41.4%** overlapped the top 10, and **29.8%** of AIO reference domains appeared nowhere on the corresponding first page. citeturn38view4turn39view3turn39view1

**Source absorption — Strong evidence.** The same study decomposed AIOs into **98,020 atomic claims** and found **11.0%** were unsupported by the cited pages, with **omission** the dominant failure mode rather than direct contradiction. Crucially, the paper also found source quality and claim fidelity were largely independent. In plain terms: Google can cite relatively credible sources and still produce unsupported claims in the summary layer. citeturn39view2turn39view0

**Ranking versus citation differences — Strong evidence.** This is one of the best-supported conclusions in the whole report. Officially, Google says AI features rely on RAG and query fan-out from the index. Empirically, the AIO study found nearly **30%** of cited domains were off-page relative to the observed first page; Ahrefs’ 2026 analysis of **863,000 SERPs** and **4 million AIO URLs** found only about **38%** of AIO-cited URLs also appeared in the top 10 for the same query, down from about **76%** in Ahrefs’ earlier 2025 measurement. Taken together, these sources support the claim that AIO citations are related to search ranking but are **not** just a restatement of visible SERP order. citeturn12view5turn39view1turn12view0

**Trust signals, authority, reviews, structured data, freshness, content structure — Proven for some factors, weak for others.** Google’s official position is clear: **standard SEO still applies**; there is **no special AI schema**; **structured data is not required**; and site owners should focus on **helpful, reliable, people-first** content, crawlability, and technical clarity. Google also explicitly says Merchant Center feeds and **Google Business Profiles** can help products and local businesses appear in AI responses, and its new **Preferred Sources** feature can visually highlight publishers a user has chosen in both AI Mode and AI Overviews. On the empirical side, the 2026 source-coverage paper found that LLM-search-selected pages tend to be **more readable**, use **more semantic HTML tags**, have slightly deeper structure, and come from somewhat less popular domains than traditional search results. What remains unproven is a direct causal weight for **backlinks**, **review counts**, or generic **schema markup** in AIO or AI Mode citation selection. citeturn37view1turn37view3turn37view4turn37view2turn20view2turn37view0turn32view4turn32view5

**Academic papers and datasets — Strong evidence.** The best public work is **Measuring Google AI Overviews** (55,393 queries), **How Generative AI Disrupts Search** (11,500-query benchmark, 14,212 total queries analysed with extensions), and **Source Coverage and Citation Bias** (very large multi-engine dataset with **414,524** AI Mode citations and **182,541** Gemini citations in that snapshot). Google’s own first-party reporting is also improving through Search Console’s new generative AI reports and Merchant Center AI performance insights announced in May–June 2026. citeturn38view4turn9view4turn31view0turn12view3turn20view3

**Well-supported claims.** Google AI search features use RAG plus query fan-out from the Search index; they prefer relatively credible sources and suppress some UGC compared with organic first-page results; they frequently cite sources that do not appear on the visible first page; and good source quality alone does not eliminate unsupported claims in the generated answer. citeturn12view5turn39view3turn39view1turn39view0

**Speculative or unsupported claims.** The exact role of backlinks, generic schema, brand mentions, and review volume in AIO/AI Mode citation selection remains opaque. Some selection behaviour may include undisclosed editorial or safety controls, but outsiders cannot quantify them. citeturn37view3turn38view4

**Gemini**

**Retrieval mechanism — Proven.** Google’s Gemini API documents **Grounding with Google Search** as a tool that lets the model decide whether search is needed, generate one or more search queries, process the results, and return text with inline `url_citation` annotations plus `google_search_call` and `google_search_result` steps. That is the cleanest public description of Gemini’s grounded citation pipeline. It also means “Gemini” needs to be split analytically into at least two behaviours: **ungrounded model output** and **Google Search-grounded output**. citeturn36view4

**Commonly cited sources — Strong evidence, but surface-specific.** In the large 2025–2026 source-coverage study, browser-based Gemini cited external websites relatively sparsely; **38%** of responses contained **no cited websites**, and among MBFC-listed domains, **Wikipedia accounted for 41%** of citations. That is a measured snapshot of one Gemini search surface, not a universal rule for every Gemini feature. citeturn32view6turn32view2

**Citation selection — Strong evidence.** Google’s API docs prove that Gemini returns text-span-level annotations when grounded. Independent measurement then shows that Gemini does **not** simply mirror either Google SERP or AI Overviews. In **How Generative AI Disrupts Search**, source overlap between SERP, AIO, and Gemini was low, with URL-level **Jaccard similarities between 0.11 and 0.18** and lower consistency than traditional Google Search across runs, devices, and locations. citeturn36view4turn9view5

**Source absorption — Limited evidence.** I did not find a clean public Gemini-only absorption study equivalent to the ChatGPT/Google/Perplexity proxy work. The best adjacent evidence comes from the broader cross-platform absorption paper, which groups **Google AI Overview/Gemini** together and finds broader citation selection but lower mean per-source absorption than ChatGPT. That is useful but not enough to isolate Gemini the consumer assistant. citeturn40view1

**Ranking versus citation differences — Strong evidence.** Ahrefs’ **15,000-prompt** analysis found Gemini’s citations had low overlap with Google’s top 10, around the same weak range as ChatGPT. The academic comparison of SERP, AIO, and Gemini also found strong divergence in source sets. So the supported conclusion is that grounded Gemini is connected to Google Search, but its displayed citations still differ materially from ordinary ranked results. citeturn13view1turn9view5

**Trust signals, authority, freshness, structure, schema, reviews, backlinks — Mixed evidence.** What is proven is that grounded Gemini is built to improve **factual accuracy**, **recency**, and **citation visibility** through Google Search. What is strong but cross-platform is that more readable, more semantically structured pages tend to be favoured by LLM-based systems relative to classic search. What is not publicly established is any Gemini-specific causal weighting for backlinks, schema, reviews, or brand mentions. citeturn36view4turn32view4turn32view5

**Academic papers and datasets — Strong evidence.** The best datasets for Gemini are the **11,500-query** benchmark and the larger source-coverage dataset with **182,541** cited Gemini URLs in one snapshot. Search Arena and the news-source study are also relevant for Google-provider models more broadly, but they are not a Gemini-only measurement set. citeturn9view4turn31view0turn25view0

**Well-supported claims.** Grounded Gemini automatically searches, may use multiple queries, exposes citation annotations, and cites sources that differ substantially from both Google SERP and AIO source sets. It also often shows no external citations at all on some browser surfaces. citeturn36view4turn9view5turn32view6

**Speculative or unsupported claims.** Outsiders do not know how often consumer Gemini answers are grounded versus ungrounded, how much weight Google ranking signals carry in citation display, or whether Wikipedia-heavy citation patterns generalise beyond the measured snapshot. citeturn32view2turn36view4

**Claude**

**Retrieval mechanism — Proven where applicable.** Anthropic’s public documentation says Claude’s web search tool gives direct access to real-time web content, can decide when search is useful, can perform **multiple progressive searches**, and can use **dynamic filtering** so Claude writes and executes code to discard irrelevant search results before they reach context. Anthropic also exposes `max_uses`, localisation, and domain allow/block controls. What Anthropic does **not** publicly disclose is the underlying search provider or ranking backend. citeturn34view0turn33view3turn33view4

**Commonly cited sources — Limited evidence.** Anthropic documents how citations are returned, but I did not find a public large-scale dataset isolating Claude web-search outputs by source type in the way geo-citation-lab or Search Arena do for OpenAI/Google/Perplexity. So, for Claude specifically, the distribution of commonly cited domains remains largely unknown outside Anthropic. citeturn33view3turn34view1

**Citation selection — Proven on interface, weak on source mix.** Anthropic states that citations are **always enabled** for web search and that each citation includes URL, title, encrypted reference, and up to 150 characters of cited text. That is proven. But no public Anthropic document explains the weighting of authority, freshness, domain type, or ranking signals in final citation selection. citeturn33view2turn33view3

**Source absorption — Limited evidence.** The best adjacent public evidence comes from **Cited but Not Verified**, where Claude models performed strongly on factual support in deep-research style citation tasks: **Claude Opus 4.5** had the highest **Fact Check** score in that model benchmark. That is informative about Claude-family model behaviour under citation-heavy agent prompts, but it is not a clean consumer-Claude web-search measurement. citeturn24view2

**Ranking versus citation differences — Unknown.** I did not find a robust public Claude-versus-SERP overlap study comparable to the Ahrefs ChatGPT/Gemini/Perplexity work. So any confident claim here would be overreach. citeturn33view3turn34view1

**Trust signals, authority, freshness, structure, schema, reviews, backlinks — Mostly unknown.** Anthropic proves freshness controls, domain controls, and citation plumbing. Cross-model evidence from citation-evaluation work suggests that selective citation can help factual support. But there is no public Claude-specific evidence quantifying the role of schema markup, backlinks, reviews, or brand mentions in source choice. citeturn34view0turn33view3turn24view2

**Academic papers and datasets — Limited.** The most relevant public paper is **Cited but Not Verified**, which includes several Claude models. Security papers on prompt injection and plugin manipulation also involve Claude-family systems, but those are about vulnerability, not normal source-ranking behaviour. There is currently no public Search Arena equivalent focused on Claude search outputs. citeturn24view2turn27search1

**Well-supported claims.** Claude can search progressively, always returns citations for web search, uses dynamic filtering, and lets administrators constrain domains. citeturn34view0turn33view3turn33view4

**Speculative or unsupported claims.** Almost every colourful GEO claim made online about Claude’s citation preferences is speculative, because the public evidence for Claude-specific source selection remains thin. citeturn34view1turn24view2

## Current Consensus

The clearest current consensus is that **AI search is retrieval-augmented but not ranking-equivalent**. Across Google AI Overviews, Gemini, ChatGPT Search, and Perplexity, public evidence consistently shows that cited sources often differ substantially from the visible top results for the same query. Google officially discloses **query fan-out** and RAG from its index; OpenAI discloses query rewriting and multiple search providers; Anthropic discloses progressive search and dynamic filtering; Perplexity discloses its own hybrid retrieval stack. Independent studies then show low overlap between AI citations and traditional rankings, especially for ChatGPT and Gemini, with Perplexity somewhat closer and Google AIO somewhere in between. citeturn12view5turn21view0turn34view0turn18view0turn13view1turn39view1turn9view5

A second consensus point is that **citation count and answer influence are different things**. The 2026 citation-absorption framework and the older GEO literature both converge on the idea that visibility in generative answers cannot be reduced to “how often was I cited?” One platform may cite many sources as shallow support, while another may cite few sources but rely on them more heavily in answer construction. citeturn40view4turn29view0

A third consensus point is that **source quality does not solve the whole problem**. Google AIO measurements show more credible sources than co-displayed first-page results, yet still about **11%** of claims were unsupported by cited pages. Deep-research style attribution benchmarks show that link validity and topical relevance can stay high while factual support degrades materially. In other words, “good sources were linked” and “the answer is well supported” are correlated only weakly or inconsistently. citeturn39view0turn39view2turn24view2

A fourth consensus point is that **freshness and index inclusion matter**, but are only entry conditions. Google says AI features use up-to-date pages from the Search index; OpenAI and Perplexity both distinguish search crawlers from training crawlers; Anthropic’s tool is explicitly for real-time web content. If a page is blocked from the relevant retrieval path, it is much less likely to be cited. But once a page is eligible, other factors determine whether it is selected and how much it shapes the answer. citeturn37view0turn8view3turn35view1turn34view0

A fifth consensus point is that **content structure and evidence density matter more than most of the AI-marketing discourse suggests**. Across GEO, citation-absorption, and source-coverage work, the recurring pattern is that pages with clearer headings, better modular structure, simpler readability, and extractable evidence such as definitions, numbers, comparisons, and procedures are more useful to generative systems. By contrast, there is still no comparably strong public evidence that generic schema markup, backlinks, review volume, or unlinked brand mentions have a stable direct causal effect on final citation selection across all systems. citeturn30view3turn30view1turn40view3turn32view4turn37view3

## High Confidence GEO Factors

Ranked by current evidence strength, the most defensible factors are these.

**Crawlability and retrievability.** This is the highest-confidence factor because it is documented directly by vendors. Google requires pages to be indexed and eligible for snippets; OpenAI requires that sites not block OAI-SearchBot if they want inclusion in summaries/snippets; Perplexity recommends allowing PerplexityBot for search inclusion. If the retrieval layer cannot access the page, later citation selection usually never begins. citeturn37view4turn22view2turn35view1

**Fresh, up-to-date content for time-sensitive intents.** Google explicitly grounds AI features in up-to-date indexed pages; Gemini grounding exists for recency and citation; Perplexity emphasises freshness as a core design requirement; Anthropic and OpenAI both frame web search as the path to information beyond model cut-offs. This factor is strongest for news, product changes, docs, pricing, markets, local data, and anything else that changes often. citeturn37view0turn36view4turn18view0turn34view0turn21view3

**Official, primary, and vertical-source status.** In every strong public cross-platform source-type dataset I found, **official**, **news**, and **vertical** sources dominate the citation pool. Google’s AIOs also skew toward higher-credibility sources than co-displayed first-page results. That does not mean small sites cannot win, but it does mean that pages with recognised primary-source status have a measurable selection advantage. citeturn40view1turn39view3

**Evidence-container design.** This is the strongest cross-platform content-structure finding. High-influence pages are longer, more modular, more semantically aligned, and richer in definitions, numerical facts, comparisons, code, and procedures. GEO experiments also found gains from adding statistics, quotations, citations, and improved fluency/readability. The limitation is that much of this evidence is descriptive or benchmark-based rather than live-production causal proof, but it is still stronger than the evidence for most fashionable GEO claims. citeturn40view3turn40view4turn30view3turn30view1

**Readable, semantically structured pages.** The source-coverage study found that LLM-based search systems favour pages with higher reading ease, lower grade level, and more semantic HTML structure than traditional search does. That does not prove “write simple and you will be cited”, but it strongly suggests that machine-legible, semantically organised documents are easier to retrieve and reuse. citeturn32view4turn32view5

**Product/local data feeds where Google explicitly supports them.** For Google specifically, Merchant Center feeds, Google Business Profiles, and user-selected Preferred Sources are real, documented levers for visibility in AI search surfaces. These are not generic cross-platform GEO factors; they are Google-specific, official mechanisms. citeturn37view2turn20view2turn20view3

**Not high-confidence yet:** backlinks, generic schema markup, review volume, brand mentions, and “LLMs.txt”. Google explicitly says there is no special AI schema and that structured data is not required for generative AI visibility. Backlinks and mentions may still matter indirectly through classic ranking, authority, and discoverability, but I did not find high-quality public evidence establishing them as stable direct cross-platform GEO factors in the final citation layer. citeturn37view3turn12view5

## Unknowns

Nobody outside the vendors can currently know the **exact candidate pool** after query rewriting, fan-out, safety filters, deduplication, and partner-content insertion. Google confirms fan-out, OpenAI confirms multi-provider query expansion, and Anthropic confirms progressive search, but none of them expose the full candidate set considered before final citation choice. citeturn12view5turn21view0turn34view0

Nobody outside the vendors can know the **true weighting** of classic SEO signals such as backlinks, click data, reviews, historical brand authority, schema, or user engagement inside the citation-selection layer. Even when AI search systems are grounded on search indexes, ranking systems and citation systems are clearly not identical. citeturn39view1turn13view1

Nobody outside the vendors can know how often a given uncited statement came from **retrieval versus parametric memory versus partner feeds versus connected apps versus prior context**. OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, and Perplexity each expose parts of the retrieval workflow in developer tooling, but not enough to reconstruct the true contribution of every knowledge source in production answers. citeturn23view0turn36view4turn33view3turn35view0

Nobody outside the vendors can fully observe **regional experiments, UI A/B tests, safety interventions, or publisher-partner prioritisation**. Public behaviour in June 2026 is a moving target. Even the best public studies are snapshots. citeturn38view4turn34view1turn22view0

## Experiments We Can Run

A small SaaS founder can still test the strongest public theories within 30 days. The goal should be to test **eligibility**, **selection**, and **absorption-like reuse** separately rather than chasing one vanity metric.

**Experiment one: inclusion and crawlability audit.** Create or select six target pages on your site. Ensure all are indexable and accessible to Googlebot, OAI-SearchBot, and PerplexityBot; verify snippet eligibility and remove accidental blocking directives. Track whether these pages begin appearing in Google Search Console’s new generative-AI reports and whether traffic with `utm_source=chatgpt.com` appears in analytics. **Methodology:** before/after audit on the same pages; **sample size:** six pages minimum, checked daily for 30 days; **success metric:** first appearance in Google generative reports, AI referrals, and citation appearances in a fixed prompt set; **limitation:** this validates eligibility, not ranking or citation causality. This experiment is evidence-based because all three vendors document crawl/index prerequisites, and Google now provides first-party generative visibility reporting. citeturn12view3turn22view2turn35view1turn37view4

**Experiment two: evidence-container rewrite test.** Pick three existing informational pages and publish matched rewrite variants on closely related topics. Keep topic and intent constant, but improve the rewritten versions with clear H2/H3 structure, an executive summary, definitions, one comparison table, dated facts, numerical evidence, and a short step-by-step section. **Methodology:** paired comparison; **sample size:** at least three original pages versus three structured pages, measured across a panel of 30 prompts run three times each per week; **primary metric:** citation incidence and whether copied answer language aligns more closely with the rewritten page; **limitation:** live AI systems are noisy and prompt-sensitive, so use repeated observations rather than one-off checks. This experiment is supported by GEO, citation-absorption, and readability/HTML-structure findings. citeturn30view3turn30view1turn40view3turn32view4

**Experiment three: freshness test for time-sensitive queries.** Choose one topic category where recency matters for your buyers—pricing, competitor feature changes, regulations, security advisories, integrations, or changelog items. Publish brief, dated updates weekly and compare them with a comparable static evergreen page. **Methodology:** weekly update cadence for four weeks; **sample size:** at least four updates and a 20-prompt recency-focused test set; **primary metric:** whether AI systems begin citing the updated page more often for “latest”, “current”, or date-constrained prompts; **limitation:** results will vary sharply by topic because some systems trigger search far more often on recency-heavy or question-form prompts. This test is grounded in vendor docs and AIO activation studies showing strong sensitivity to question type and recency. citeturn37view0turn36view4turn20view0turn38view4

**Experiment four: primary-source versus generic-blog format.** Publish one page as a generic explainer and another as a primary-source artefact: original benchmark, transparent methodology note, product specification, security white paper, pricing methodology, or integration reference. **Methodology:** matched topic, different source posture; **sample size:** two pairs minimum, 20 prompts each; **primary metric:** citation incidence and source position across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AIO/AI Mode, and Gemini; **limitation:** you need something genuinely original to test. This follows from the strong evidence that official and vertical sources dominate citations. citeturn40view1turn39view3

**Experiment five: Google-specific local or product feed test.** If your SaaS has a local presence or product catalogue, complete Google Business Profile and, where relevant, Merchant Center data. Compare AI visibility for local and product-intent prompts before and after feed completion. **Methodology:** one before/after implementation on Google surfaces only; **sample size:** 20 local/product prompts weekly over four weeks; **primary metric:** presence in AI Overviews, AI Mode, and Search Console generative reports; **limitation:** this does not generalise to ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Claude. It is still worth doing because Google documents this path explicitly. citeturn37view2turn20view3

For all five experiments, use the same prompt panel every week. Include direct questions, open-ended questions, comparison prompts, and “latest” prompts. Save screenshots, cited URLs, and answer text. Do not judge success on one lucky citation. Judge it on repeated appearance across at least three weekly runs. That setup reflects what the reproducible public studies do: repeated measurement beats anecdote. citeturn38view4turn9view4turn10view2

## Actionable GEO Strategy for a New Website Starting From Zero

Start with **retrieval eligibility**, because it is the one part that is both proven and controllable. Make the site crawlable, indexable, and snippet-eligible. Do not block Googlebot if you want Google AI visibility; do not block OAI-SearchBot if you want ChatGPT Search visibility; and do not block PerplexityBot if you want Perplexity visibility. If you do not want model training but do want search visibility on OpenAI and Perplexity, treat training bots and search bots separately because those vendors explicitly separate them. citeturn37view4turn22view2turn8view3turn35view1

Build a small number of **official-feeling, primary-source, evidence-dense pages** before you build a large content library. The evidence points toward official, vertical, and primary-source material being cited more often than generic commodity blog posts. For a new SaaS, that usually means pages such as: product documentation, pricing explanations, security and compliance details, integration references, benchmark or methodology pages, comparison pages with transparent criteria, and dated changelog or release notes. These are the page types most likely to look like citable evidence rather than interchangeable marketing copy. citeturn40view1turn39view3turn37view1

Write each important page as an **evidence container**. Put the main answer at the top. Use clear headings that map to likely subquestions. Include at least one table, a few dated facts, one comparison block, and one short procedure or checklist where relevant. Add definitions for key terms. Keep prose readable. This recommendation is supported by the strongest cross-platform empirical work on absorption and structure; it is better supported than chasing fashionable hacks. citeturn40view3turn32view4turn30view1turn30view3

For Google specifically, use **standard SEO and supported Google data products**, not AI folklore. Implement structured data where it helps with ordinary Search rich results, but do not expect generic schema alone to unlock AI citation. If you are selling products or local services, complete Merchant Center and Google Business Profile data because Google explicitly says these can improve visibility in AI responses. If you are a publisher with an audience, use **Preferred Sources** because Google now says those preferences can be highlighted in AI Mode and AI Overviews. citeturn37view3turn37view2turn20view2

Treat **freshness** as a publishing discipline, not a one-off optimisation. Put visible dates and update pages when facts change. This matters most for product specs, regulations, market data, integrations, and anything where a query contains “latest”, “new”, current-year language, or comparison of changing tools. AI search systems were built precisely to retrieve this kind of information from the web rather than relying on stale model memory. citeturn37view0turn36view4turn34view0turn21view3

Measure outcomes with the tools that now exist. Use **Google Search Console generative-AI reporting** for Google surfaces. Track **`utm_source=chatgpt.com`** in analytics for ChatGPT referrals. Keep your own fixed prompt panel for ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AIO/AI Mode, and Gemini, and record citations weekly for 30 days. If you are not measuring repeated citation incidence, you are guessing. citeturn12view3turn22view2

Finally, avoid recommendations that are still mostly speculative. There is not enough public evidence in June 2026 to say that a new site should prioritise **LLMs.txt**, generic AI-specific schema, review manipulation, backlink schemes, or broad “brand mention” campaigns for AI citation purposes. Those may matter indirectly through classic authority and discoverability, but the public evidence base does not justify treating them as first-line GEO strategies. For a new site starting from zero, the evidence-backed path is much simpler: get crawled, be current, publish primary and structured evidence, and measure systematically. citeturn12view5turn37view3turn40view4
